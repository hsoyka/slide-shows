<h2>6 Steps to Creating Quality Metadata</h2>


<ol>

<li>Organize your information.</li>

<li>Did you write a project abstract to obtain funding for your proposal? Re-use it in your metadata! </li>

<li>Did you use a lab notebook or other notes during the data development process that define measurements and other parameters? </li>

<li>Do you have the contact information for colleagues you worked with?</li>

<li>What about citations for other data sources you used in your project?</li>

</ol>


<h2>6 Steps to Creating Quality Metadata, cont'd</h2>


<ol>

<li>Write your metadata using a metadata tool</li>

<li>Review the record for accuracy and completeness</li>

<li>Have someone else read &amp; review your record</li>

<li>Revise the record based on comments from reviewer</li>

<li>Review the record and then publish!</li>

</ol>


<h2>Tips for writing quality metadata</h2>


<ol>

<li>Do not use jargon</li>

<li>Define technical terms and acronyms</li>

<li>Clearly state data limitations (eg. completeness, omissions) and considerations for data reuse</li>

<li>Use "none" or "unknown" meaningfully

*"None" usually means that you knew about data and nothing existed (e.g., a “0” cubic feet per second discharge value)

*"Unknown" means that you don’t know whether that data existed or not (e.g., a null value)</li>

</ol>


<h2>Titles!</h2>


<ol>

<li>Titles are critical for helping others find your data

*Complete titles include: What, When, Where, Who, and Scale.

*Informative titles include: topic, timeliness of the data, specific information about place and geography</li>

</ol>


<h2>Example: Which is better?</h2>


<p>A: Rivers</p>


<p>B: Greater Yellowstone Rivers from 1:126,700 U.S.Forest Service Visitor Maps (1961-1983)</p>


<h2>More Tips for Writing Quality Metadata</h2>


<ol>

<li>Be specific! Quantify when you can.

Example: 

"We checked our work using a random sample of 5 monitoring sites, reviewed by 2 people. We determined our work to be 95% complete based on these inspections."</li>

</ol>


<h2>More Tips for Writing Quality Metadata</h2>


<ol>

<li>Select keywords wisely</li>

<li>Use descriptive &amp; clear writing</li>

<li>Fully qualify geographic locations</li>

<li>Use standard domain thesauri for keywords when possible (eg. USGS Biocomplexity Thesaurus)</li>

</ol>


<h2>More Tips for Writing Quality Metadata</h2>


<ol>

<li>Remember: a computer will read your metadata</li>

<li>Do not use symbols that could be misinterpreted </li>

<li>Do not use tabs, indents, or line feeds</li>

<li>When copying and pasting from other sources, use a text editor to eliminate hidden characters </li>

</ol>


<h2>Summary</h2>


<p>Review your final product to make sure that the documentation represents all of the information necessary to use or reuse the data.</p>


<p>Remember: a well-written title and good keywords are critical to data discovery!</p>
